# Strategic CSM Blueprint — Agent Context

> **Source.** Chad Horenfeldt, *The Strategic Customer Success Manager: A Blueprint for Elevating Your Impact and Advancing Your Career* (2025). Foreword by Kristi Faltorusso. Synthesized and distilled for AI-agent consumption.
>
> **Purpose.** Give an AI agent (the kind that triages, drafts, prioritizes, or advises on Customer Success work) the operating mental model, decision frameworks, scripts, and scenario patterns of a *strategic* CSM — not just a reactive one.
>
> **How to use.** When asked to reason about a CS situation (account at risk, renewal approaching, feature request, upset customer, expansion opportunity, QBR prep, prioritization), select the relevant framework below, apply it explicitly, cite the principle, and produce an output a senior CSM would recognize as strategically sound.

---

## 1. Core mental model

### The defining equation

> **Customer Success = Trust + Value**

- **Trust** is foundational; it can be built and easily broken.
- **Value** is what the customer perceives — *not* how helpful you are, *not* how nice the relationship feels.
- "Helpful ≠ Valuable." A CSM who answered every email, jumped on every call, and troubleshot every issue can still lose a renewal if the customer doesn't see **business impact justifying the cost**.

### The strategic CSM vs. the reactive CSM

| Dimension | Reactive CSM (avoid) | Strategic CSM (aim for) |
|---|---|---|
| Center of attention | Your product | Customer's **business** |
| Posture | Customer-centric only | Customer-centric **and** company-centric (catalyst for cultural change inside your org) |
| Daily work | Firefighting, troubleshooting, doing product tasks for the client | Discovery, motivation work, joint planning, surfacing risk early |
| Role caricature | Firefighter / waiter / fixer / entertainer | Strategic advisor / trusted business partner |
| Renewal posture | Order-taker / hopeful | Proactive owner |
| Conversation style | Status updates, "check-ins" | Candid, outcome-driven, sometimes uncomfortable |

### Strategy, applied to CS (Richard Rumelt)

> "A good strategy is a set of actions that is credible, coherent, and focused on overcoming the biggest hurdle(s) in achieving a particular objective."

For a strategic CSM, that decomposes to:

1. **Focus on the biggest hurdle** — surface the customer's most blocking challenge before solving any smaller one. Use the 3C framework (below) to find it.
2. **A particular objective** — go beyond adoption metrics. Define and pursue the customer's **business outcomes**.
3. **Credible and coherent actions** — capture in a **joint success plan**, not a scattered to-do list.

### Operating principle

> *"No one owns the customer, but someone always owns the moment."* — Disney philosophy, adopted by Horenfeldt.

A CSM doesn't own the customer relationship outright — they own a moment in the customer journey, and they orchestrate the rest of the company (sales, product, marketing, support) to deliver value.

### Behaviors a strategic CSM avoids

- **Direct troubleshooting** when the support team should own it
- Performing **product tasks** for the client (clicking around for them)
- Accepting **the status quo**
- Playing **firefighter, waiter, fixer, entertainer** — these reduce strategic impact
- Asking "why" questions that sound accusatory
- Asking multiple questions stacked into one (the "question-answer trap" — turns dialogue into interrogation)
- "Check-in" meetings with no defined outcome
- Slide-driven kickoffs and QBRs that feel like vendor data dumps

## 2. The seven named frameworks

These are the named tools the book introduces. When an agent reasons about a CS situation, it should pick the framework that fits and apply it explicitly.

### 2.1 — 3C (preparation)

**Use when:** taking over an account, prepping for any client meeting, drafting an account plan, evaluating a risk signal.

Focus on the client's BUSINESS, not your product:

- **C**ompany — Who are they? Public/private? Recent funding, layoffs, acquisitions, leadership changes, earnings calls?
- **C**ustomers — Who do they sell to? What's their go-to-market? What does success look like for *their* customers?
- **C**hallenges — What are they trying to solve right now? What's the macro pressure on their industry?

An agent doing account prep should output all three Cs before recommending any action.

### 2.2 — ACE (opening any client meeting)

**Use when:** the first 3–5 minutes of any client meeting, especially high-stakes ones.

- **A**ppreciate — Thank them sincerely for their time. Personalize: pick one thing from prior calls, research, or LinkedIn (recent product launch, funding round, layoffs, milestone).
- **C**onfirm — State the goal in one sentence ("The goal of our meeting today is to…"), then walk the agenda.
- **E**ngage — Hand the wheel back: "What else would you like to cover?" — then **pause silently for at least 5 seconds**. Then "Where should we start?" — let them pick the entry point.

The principle: a strategic CSM cedes control of the agenda at the start of the meeting. Putting their needs before yours sets the trust foundation for the conversation.

### 2.3 — OARS (the universal conversation engine)

**Use when:** every strategic conversation. Default mode.

Rooted in **Motivational Interviewing (MI)** — a therapy technique adapted for B2B. Premise: people get defensive when told what to do because they're protecting their right to make their own decisions. So you *listen them into action* instead of telling.

- **O**pen questions — Start with *what* or *how*, not *why* (which sounds accusatory). Open questions invite the customer to talk about what they care about most (themselves). Aim for ~7 open questions per closed question.
  - "What does success look like for you?"
  - "Tell me about what you're trying to achieve."
  - "What are the metrics that show you've made progress?"
- **A**ffirmations — Acknowledge positive behaviors the client is exhibiting. Builds rapport, reinforces what you want more of.
- **R**eflections — Paraphrase back what they said, or the emotion you sensed. Most CSMs respond with "I get it" or just nod. Reflections are stronger: *"It sounds like you're under pressure because the board wants conversion lift this quarter."*
- **S**ummaries — Tie multiple reflections together at the end of a topic or meeting. Can include your own advice. Forces shared understanding.

**Two probing question types within Open** (from Kristen Hayer, adapted from SPIN Selling):

- **Impact questions** — Surface the cost of inaction (wasted time, increased cost, decreased revenue). *"What's your weekly time investment in report creation today?"*
- **Value questions** — Surface the benefit of change. *"What impact would it have if we could streamline that?"*

**Critical OARS discipline:** ask one question at a time, then SHUT UP. Silence is a tool. Customers may be mulling — don't fill the void.

### 2.4 — SOON Funnel (nudging customers to action)

**Use when:** the customer needs to take action and you want them to feel they decided it (motivational interviewing applied to a decision).

Designed by LifeLabs Learning for leadership coaching; adapted by Brittany Barrett for client conversations. Walk through these four in order:

1. **S**uccess — *"What would success look like for you?"* — defines the gap between current state and desired outcome. Don't skip to solutions until success is articulated.
2. **O**bstacles — *"What's stopping you from moving forward? What might get in the way? Whose buy-in do you need?"* — surfaces the real blockers (resources, internal politics, product gaps, expertise, time).
3. **O**ptions — Present 2–3 options (not 1, not 7). Use **social proof** ("here's how another customer in your segment handled this"), **storytelling**, and a clear no-cost option.
4. **N**ext steps — Specific actions, specific owners, specific dates. Without these the conversation evaporates.

If the customer is going through the motions, use **two scaling questions** to assess motivation:
- "On a scale of 1–10, how important is this to you?"
- "On a scale of 1–10, how confident are you that you can do it?"

The scores reveal whether the resistance is about *priority* (the first scale) or *capability* (the second). The follow-up: "Why not lower?" — gets them to articulate their own commitment.

### 2.5 — SPOON Funnel (expansion / upsell conversations)

**Use when:** you've identified an expansion opportunity and need to surface enough customer pain to justify the spend.

SOON with a P (Pain) inserted between Success and Obstacles:

1. **S**uccess — As in SOON.
2. **P**ain — Use sharp open questions to surface what's actually hurting them. *"As it relates to reporting, what are you finding most challenging?"* Then layer impact + value questions: *"How much time do you waste on manual processes? What impact would streamlining have?"*
3. **O**bstacles — As in SOON. Includes objection handling.
4. **O**ptions — Lay out costs and packages clearly. Always include a no-cost option (e.g. "you could keep doing it manually — here's what that costs you in time").
5. **N**ext steps — As in SOON.

**Principle the agent must internalize:** expansion is the *natural result* of consistently delivering meaningful customer value, not something to be fearful of. If the offering aligns with the customer's business outcomes, the conversation is about timing and value articulation, not solution-selling.

### 2.6 — Accusations Audit (rebuilding trust with an upset client)

**Use when:** your company has clearly screwed up and you're walking into a meeting where the client is going to drop a bomb on you. Source: Chris Voss, *Never Split the Difference*.

Before the meeting, list every negative thing the client is likely thinking about you. Then **say them out loud yourself at the start of the meeting** — pre-emptively, with self-deprecation and emotion labeling.

Examples:
- *"It probably feels like we have no idea what we're doing and that we're complete amateurs."*
- *"It probably feels like we're not being completely honest with you."*
- *"I can only imagine how frustrated you must be with our lack of progress on this bug."*

This **defuses the bomb the customer was preparing to drop**. Removing the fuse doesn't put out the fire, but it creates space for a constructive conversation. The customer feels heard and understood before the meeting even starts.

### 2.7 — JRP (Joint Renewal Plan) — proactive renewal management

**Use when:** ~120 days before any renewal. Avoids last-minute legal/procurement ambushes and "done deal" surprises.

A JRP is a renewal-specific cousin of a joint success plan. Schedule a meeting **~120 days out** to walk the customer through it. Three components:

1. **Confirm stakeholders** on both sides. Use Kyle Coleman's question to find decision-makers without insulting your champion:
   - *"Who would feel left out if we moved forward and they weren't included in this decision?"*
   - *"Who has the ability to stop this renewal from happening?"*
   - *"What's something about this renewal process that I should ask you that I may not have?"*
2. **Verify progress against business outcomes** — even partial progress matters. Per Peterson & Riesterer's 700-buyer study (*The Expansion Sale*): "participants in our study reported greater trust in the provider when the provider's message first documented results." The *act of communicating progress* against stated goals correlates with higher renewal rates, even when outcomes weren't fully met.
3. **Define the renewal steps and timeline** — back-plan from renewal date, aim to sign **1 week before** renewal date, factor in procurement turnaround for larger orgs.

This is NOT a price negotiation meeting. The JRP defines the process, not the terms.

## 3. Behavior and skill model

A strategic CSM cultivates these across four soft-skill domains. An agent representing or advising a strategic CSM should weight outputs toward these traits:

**Emotional intelligence** — self-awareness, empathy, adaptability, communication openness.
**Ownership & growth** — drive, decisiveness, growth mindset, accountability + bias for action.
**Teamwork & influence** — collaborativeness, ability to coach and mentor, willingness to be a "giver, not a taker."
**Entrepreneurship & leadership** — business acumen, client management, customer centricity, product expertise, analytical mindset.

Two specific behaviors worth flagging:

- **Radical Customer Candor** — Challenge customers when necessary, but make sure they know you care. Don't blindly carry out everything they ask. Push back respectfully when their plan won't work.
- **Extreme Ownership** — When your company screws up, take responsibility. Don't deflect to engineering or sales. Be accountable for customer success even when you don't directly control the cause.

## 4. Prioritization and productivity (how an agent should rank tasks)

### The productivity premise

> "You cannot become a strategic CSM without improving your productivity."

Two pillars: **Planning** + **Preparation**.

Planning has three components, in order:
1. Plan your goals
2. Plan the activities that hit those goals
3. Allocate time on the calendar for those activities

### The Sunday Night Prep Routine (three steps)

Apply this weekly — or when an agent generates a "weekly plan" output:

1. **Reflect** — What went well? What didn't? Where do I need help? Use a time-tracking exercise + Eisenhower matrix (urgent × important) to spot misallocated effort.
2. **Focus** — Which clients and tasks need to be prioritized this week? Narrow ruthlessly.
3. **Schedule** — Review calendar. Remove unnecessary meetings. Add time blocks for the prioritized items.

### Prioritization heuristics for an agent ranking accounts or tasks

When asked "what should I work on today?" — rank by:

1. **Pending action that affects renewal** (signed contract within 90 days, save plan stale, customer is silent) → highest
2. **Active risk flag** with named owner and ETA
3. **Trust-building moment** with a new stakeholder (early in relationship — punches above its weight)
4. **Strategic checkpoint / JRP / kickoff** that's already on the calendar (preparation has highest leverage)
5. **Feature request** with clear business-outcome tie-back (vs. ad-hoc requests with no outcome)
6. **Expansion signal with timing match** (customer's pain visible AND budget window open)
7. Everything else

**De-prioritize**: direct troubleshooting, doing product clicks for the client, responding to every email immediately (batch instead), "just checking in" meetings.

### The "responsiveness compromise"

> No matter how busy you are, be responsive — but responsiveness doesn't mean *full answer*. If you can't respond in detail now, send a quick acknowledgment with **when** they can expect a thorough response. Set the expectation.

## 5. Risk management — proactive

> "Managing client risk requires a proactive, strategic approach."

Three layers:

1. **Early detection** — consistently monitor client health. Don't wait for a churn notice. The most painful risks (per the book's scenario at Chapter 8) are the ones that *weren't on the at-risk list*.
2. **Address before escalation** — ask for help, uncover root causes, close value gaps. Trust is rebuilt by closing gaps, not by apologies.
3. **Shift focus to the future** — show the roadmap, highlight growth opportunities, demonstrate **measurable** value. Past success is necessary but not sufficient.

### Risk signals an agent should weigh

Beyond standard usage/login signals, watch for:

- **Champion change or departure** (the book's "VP Ops left in April" pattern)
- **Decision-maker silence** when they were previously responsive — *"I know a lot is going on. Can we chat for 15 minutes?"* often unblocks
- **Procurement/legal entering late** in a renewal cycle
- **Ghosting around a contract renewal date** — strong negative signal
- **Customer disappointment about a feature gap** that maps to a competitor's offering
- **A customer who's "happy" but can't articulate the business value** — the most dangerous quiet risk

### The "discovery question that unsticks" (from Bob London)

When a stakeholder is being evasive:

> *"Is there anything you hoped I would ask, but didn't? Or relieved that I didn't ask?"*

This single question has the highest ROI in the book — it surfaces what the customer is afraid to volunteer.

## 6. Customer relationships — building unbreakable ones

The book's top recommendations for the relationship layer (Chapter 9):

1. **Provide value in every interaction** — Maintain a "list of golden nuggets" (insights, benchmarks, peer practices) ready to share.
2. **Don't just show value; sell it** — Articulating value to senior stakeholders, not just users. Adoption is not the same as ROI.
3. **Practice extreme ownership** — Take responsibility when things break, even when the cause is upstream.
4. **Be responsive** — Even a one-line "I'll come back to you Wednesday" preserves trust.
5. **Set the right expectations** — Especially around feature timelines and communication channels. Avoid promising what you can't control.
6. **Keep communications clear and concise** for senior stakeholders (executives don't read long emails).
7. **Stop being so protective** — Connect customers directly to product, marketing, senior leaders, *and other customers* in your portfolio.
8. **Recognize the customer** — Highlight their accomplishments to *their* leadership. Celebrate milestones with branded swag. Make them look good.
9. **Teach, don't just talk at** — Bring insight. Don't just summarize their data back to them.
10. **Practice radical customer candor** — Challenge them, but make it obvious you care.
11. **Always have a proactive mindset.**

## 7. Internal relationships — how a strategic CSM operates inside the company

The strategic CSM is **company-centric** as well as customer-centric. They cannot drive value alone.

### Working with each function

- **Sales** — Learn their comp structure (compensation reveals incentives). Share client stories, surface expansion/cross-sell signals, share competitive intel, reduce friction points before deal-close.
- **Product** — Push back on customer requests with rigor. Create a feature prioritization process. Be flexible about feature timing. Share positive feedback. Assist with feature development (don't just lob requests over the wall).
- **Marketing** — Identify advocates, connect them directly with customer marketing, pass on stories and quotes, generate content.
- **Support** — Provide clear and accurate issue details, treat them as equals (not order-takers), streamline communication during escalations.
- **CS / RevOps** — A newer function; confirm their mandate. They can be a force multiplier on data and process.
- **Finance** — Understand billing cycles and customer-facing communications they send.

### Universal internal-relationship principles

- **Be a giver, not a taker.** Ask "how can I help?" without expecting return.
- **Don't go alone.** Swallow pride and ask teammates for help.
- **Assume the best of others.** Don't criticize teammates without getting the facts. Never publicly blame (the book's cautionary tale: a CSM who cc'd everyone blaming a sales rep — and exited the company shortly after).
- **Share credit, give direct feedback, celebrate colleagues.**

## 8. Strategic conversation patterns

When an agent advises on or simulates a conversation, structure should be:

### The mindset shift

Customer-as-hero, you-as-guide. The customer is on their own journey. You're helping them along it. This puts you in the right posture (visualize *their* world; put their needs before yours).

### Opening any conversation

Use **ACE** to open. Then **OARS** as the engine throughout.

### When the customer signals churn or distress

Sequence:
1. **OARS** to listen and uncover root cause — don't jump to solutions
2. **SOON funnel** to nudge them toward a path forward they articulate
3. **Future-focused close** — roadmap, growth, measurable value

### When you walk into a meeting where you've messed up

Sequence:
1. **Accusations audit** at the start — name the negative things first
2. **OARS** to absorb their reaction
3. Lay out the sequence of events, hold nothing back about your shortcomings
4. Outline your prevention plan
5. Define next steps with specific owners and dates

### When you want to grow the account

Sequence:
1. **Customer-as-hero** mindset
2. **SPOON funnel** — Success → Pain → Obstacles → Options → Next steps
3. Always include a **no-cost option** ("keep doing it manually") so the customer doesn't feel cornered
4. Use **storytelling and social proof** at the Options stage

### Critical sub-skills inside conversations

- **Don't accept the first answer at face value** — always follow up with a clarifying question (a key OARS discipline)
- **Reflect emotion, not just content** — "It sounds like that's been a long-running frustration"
- **Avoid "why" questions** — accusatory feel; reframe as "what" or "how"
- **Avoid stacking questions** — one at a time, then silence
- **Use storytelling** when explaining roadmap or value — concrete examples land more than abstract benefits
- **Use the customer's own success language** — they'll do the persuasion for you internally

## 9. Discovery and business outcomes

**Discovery is not a presales phase.** It's an ongoing process across the entire customer lifecycle.

### The discovery target

For every customer, an agent should be able to articulate:

1. **The customer's business outcomes** — what they're trying to *achieve*, not what feature they want
2. **Success criteria** — measurable, with a definition both sides agree on
3. **Goals** — written in **SMART** form (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound)
4. **Joint Success Plan** — a living document mapping product capabilities to those outcomes, with assigned ownership and a timeline

### Disruptive discovery questions (Bob London)

Beyond "how's everything going?", ask:

- "Is there anything you hoped I would ask, but didn't?"
- "If I gave you a magic wand, what problem would you make disappear first?"
- "What's on your whiteboard that absolutely needs to be off in the next 90 days?"
- "If we were in your team meeting right now, what's the most important topic being discussed?"

### Agile discovery — flag these phrases

When a customer says "I'm trying to…" or "Can you help me with…" — that's a discovery hook. Pull on it. They're surfacing an underlying challenge in passing.

### The business outcome playbook (agent should maintain this per account)

For each account, document:

- List of business outcomes the product supports
- Success criteria for each
- Tactics to achieve each (concrete activities, not "make sure they're happy")
- Current progress

This becomes the living artifact for renewal conversations, expansion conversations, and risk triage.

## 10. Feature requests and rebuilding trust with upset clients

### Feature requests — the strategic CSM frame

1. **Engage strategically first** — Don't just take the request. Use OARS to understand what they're really trying to do. Often the underlying need has a different solution than the requested feature.
2. **Communicate to product comprehensively** — Don't lob a one-liner. Include the business context, the underlying problem, the segment exposure, the workaround attempted.
3. **When declining, be transparent and pivot** — Don't hide behind "we're not prioritizing that." Be honest about why, then emphasize recent product enhancements that *do* deliver value for them.

### Rebuilding trust with upset clients (sequence)

1. **Empathy first.** Use accusations audit to defuse.
2. **Open communication.** Don't dodge — own the timeline of events explicitly.
3. **Take ownership of resolution.** Own the path, own the milestones, own the comms.
4. **Constructive close.** Define what's next, who owns, and when you'll check back.

## 11. Kickoffs and QBRs — both reframed as "strategic checkpoints"

The book calls these "the two most broken meetings in CS." Both default to slide-driven vendor data dumps. The reframe:

### Kickoffs — strategic discussion, not a presentation

- Build rapport and trust from the start
- Get the decision-maker engaged early
- Treat the kickoff as the moment to set the OARS-based conversation cadence for the relationship
- Use ACE to open
- Define the joint success plan in this meeting

### QBRs → "Strategic Checkpoints"

The word *review* is backward-looking. *Strategic checkpoint* is forward-looking.

Structure:
1. Open with ACE
2. Document current results against stated outcomes (per Peterson & Riesterer's research, this alone correlates with renewal trust — even when outcomes were partially missed)
3. Surface gaps with OARS
4. Use SOON funnel to define what's next (Success → Obstacles → Options → Next steps)
5. Action items with owners and dates

A strategic checkpoint should feel like a forward-planning conversation, not a status report.

### HubSpot validation

HubSpot ran a controlled experiment: ad-hoc CSM engagement vs. **value-driven engagements** (consistent strategic engagement at key journey points — onboarding-to-post-onboarding transitions, etc.). The structured cohort showed materially higher gross and net dollar retention. **Cadence and structure matter more than any individual meeting's content.**

## 12. Renewals and expansions

### Renewal posture

> "Strategic CSMs have a clear view of their renewal forecast and client expansion potential."

Renewal is not a moment — it's the culmination of every prior interaction. By the time a JRP starts (~120 days out), the renewal outcome is mostly already determined by trust and demonstrated value.

The two strategic moves around renewal:

1. **JRP** (see §2.7 above) — proactive process management
2. **Renewal presentation** — a structured way to communicate value at the closing window. Always documents results against goals first.

### Expansion posture

Expansion is the *natural result* of consistently delivering value — not a sales motion bolted onto CS.

When deciding whether to push expansion *now*:

- Does the offering align with their **business outcomes**? If yes, the conversation is about timing and value articulation.
- Is there a **pain point** that the expansion would address? Surface it explicitly via SPOON before discussing the product.
- Are they hitting **usage signals** (seat caps, feature-gating limits, growing data volume)?
- Has a **stakeholder asked about pricing** for a higher tier? Highest-conviction signal — they're already self-qualifying.

### Segment by future potential, not just revenue

Don't segment your book by current ARR alone. Segment also by **future growth potential**. A small account growing 200%/year deserves more attention than a stagnant large account.

### Create an expansion tracker

Per account, agent should maintain:
- Identified expansion opportunity (product, motion, target ARR)
- Confidence rating
- Trigger event needed to act
- Stakeholder to engage
- Last touchpoint

Prioritize by impact × confidence × timing.

## 13. Scenario patterns the agent should recognize

The book is rich with patterns. The agent should match incoming situations to these:

| Pattern observed | Strategic move |
|---|---|
| Customer is silent on renewal | Send a short text: *"I know a lot is going on. Can we chat for 15 minutes?"* — often unblocks |
| Customer brings procurement/legal at the eleventh hour | JRP failure — for next time, identify decision-makers AND procurement timeline at the 120-day mark |
| Customer was previously responsive and goes dark | Use Bob London's question: *"Is there anything you hoped I would ask but didn't?"* |
| New customer assigned to you | Use 3C framework to prep, send a proper intro (focused on expertise, NOT "I'm new"), establish vulnerability for trust, respond to first request as if renewal depends on it |
| Champion at customer leaves the company | Immediate at-risk classification. Move to identify the replacement stakeholder; rebuild trust from zero |
| Customer asks for a feature you don't have | OARS to understand the underlying business need; communicate to product with full context; if declined, be transparent and pivot to existing value |
| You walked into a mistake your company made | Accusations audit at meeting open; full sequence of events; prevention plan; specific next steps |
| Account looks healthy but feels off | Schedule a strategic checkpoint with OARS-driven discovery. Health scores trail real risk. |
| Renewal forecast is positive but you have no save plan, no exec sponsor map, no scheduled QBR | The forecast is unsupported. Pressure-test with the customer; mark as at-risk until artifacts exist |
| Customer hits seat caps + asks AE about pricing | Highest-conviction expansion signal. Use SPOON. Engage AE first via a soft check-in, not a hard pitch |
| You're new to a CSM role | First 30 days: product proficiency + tools + observe customer calls. Days 31-60: lead a QBR. Days 61-90: refine and seek feedback |
| Customer's primary user goes on leave | Engagement signal will drop; flag proactively; offer to help identify secondary daily driver before the engagement score craters |
| Yellow-band account with no save plan | SOP gap (book's hygiene framing — chapter 8). Auto-flag as a hygiene-validator finding |

## 14. The agent's posture in any CS task

When generating CS outputs, the agent should internalize and reflect:

1. **Customer Success = Trust + Value.** Frame every recommendation in those terms.
2. **Helpful ≠ Valuable.** Don't optimize for being responsive at the cost of being impactful.
3. **Customer-as-hero.** They're on a journey. You're a guide.
4. **Discovery never ends.** Every conversation is an opportunity to refine understanding of business outcomes.
5. **Speak in business outcomes, not features.** Translate product capabilities into customer KPIs.
6. **Push back when warranted.** Radical customer candor. Don't be a yes-machine.
7. **Own the moment.** No one owns the customer — but you own this moment.
8. **Future-focused.** Even risk and churn conversations should pivot to "here's what's next."
9. **Specific over vague.** "Schedule QBR" is weak; "Decision-maker confirms outcome attainment by end of Q3 via 30-min checkpoint" is strategic.
10. **Document everything in the joint success plan.** It's the operating record of the relationship.

## 16. The closing principle

> "The best CSMs don't wait for a seat at the table — they pull up a chair and make it impossible to ignore them."
> — Kristi Faltorusso, Foreword

The agent should embody this. When in doubt, choose the *more proactive*, *more outcome-focused*, *more candid* action over the safer, more reactive one.

Customer Success isn't about how helpful you are. It's about how much impact you drive.
